PROLOGUE: OVERVIEW

Roles + Deliverables + Tools

I led the design of Virtual Musem’s platform (both the exhibits and website) on desktop applications, including efforts to improve the platform through user research, prototype mockups with Figma, and usability tests. Deliverables included images, icons, prototype mockups of the app, logos, and a style guide.

What is Virtual Museum?

Virtual Museum brings you high quality immersive experiences on a range of topics based on art and history. Explore anything from the history of vampiric lore to Pop Art on Virtual Museum. These exhibits are personal, interactive, and engaging. We will explore how I created the exhibit on Francisco Goya in this case study.

Problem

Curious young professionals want interactive ways to experience art and culture from their homes.

+ Solution

Curious young professionals can explore art and culture without leaving their homes with a virtual interactive museum on art and history.

PART 1: DISCOVERY + RESEARCH

CHAPTER 1: The Survey

Using a survey I conducted of 16 participants, I found that the majority of users want the following interactive features in the exhibit:

  • Trivia questions asking about art or history concept
  • Open-ended questions asking about art or history concepts

Almost all survey takers want to see how other people engage with the interactive features as well.

CHAPTER 2: The Competitive Analysis

A competitive analysis of Google Arts and Culture and the British Museum showed a lot of opportunities in the visual aspects of Virtual Museum.

Google Arts and Culture has a lot of fun, interactive, and informative content that inspired the Goya exhibit. Zooming in on certain features on a painting, the scroll journey, content readability, and engaging writing are things that Google Arts and Culture does well.

However, one drawback I noticed with Google Arts and Culture that could benefit Virtual Museum was offering content on a website rather than an app. Only offering content on an app could deter users.

British Museum’s card design and layout was a strength I used for the cards in Future and Past Exhibits.

PART 2: INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

CHAPTER 4: The User Stories

It was important to keep user stories as simple as possible since the product was about a user journey through art and history. Overcomplicating this would defeat the purpose of Virtual Museum. Simplicity as well as the needs I identified through personas, the survey, and competitive analysis produced the following user stories for the MVP:

  • high priority: Entering exhibit
  • high priority: Interaction based on trivia questions
  • high priority: Interaction based on open-ended questions
  • high priority: Interaction based on viewing other people’s responses
  • medium priority: Accessing future exhibits

PART 3: BRANDING

CHAPTER 8: The Logo + Colors + Typography

Virtual Museum’s brand intersects art with technology. It combines the old with the new to transform experiences and make visitors engage more deeply with the exhibits.

I chose the name Virtual Museum because to capture a real museum essence. Even though the tone of the site is lighthearted, the brand still conveys an academic feel. The logo draws upon classic museum logos that exist. It is sleek and minimal with a lot of authority.

The exhibits are fun and interactive, and the logo complements this by showing that the site is also a serious educational platform, like real museums. Playfair Display and PT Mono complement each other. They show that there’s a place for technology and history to interact.

The brand is two-fold: internal and external. The internal brand is sleek, minimal, and black and white so the exhibit branding can take precedence. This external color branding will be based on the specific exhibit at the time, but the typography stays the same.

Internal + External Branding

The internal branding, the branding for Virtual Museum's website, is minimalist to give precedence to the branding of the exhibits. "Chiaroscuro" and "Etching" are subtle enough to give more presence to the exhibits.

The external brand colors for the Goya exhibit come from some of his most popular paintings to match the atmosphere of the portraits that the exhibit showcases.

PART 4: VISUAL DESIGN + PROTOTYPE/TESTING

CHAPTER 10: The High-Fidelity Mockups

I faced two big challenges when designing the mockups:

  • the scroll format of the Goya exhibit
  • the arrows to indicate the next screen (discussed in testing)

I originally wanted the user to infinitely scroll down the exhibit because it’s simple, easy, and they are used to scroll journeys. Figuring out how to format the scroll journey was the tricky part. Did users want the Goya exhibit to be one continuous page or did they want to take it page by page?

In the end, I decided that scrolling page by page would make the information much easier to digest and not overwhelm users as much. This format also makes the interactive components like trivia more engaging.

CHAPTER 11: The Preference Test

Once I figured that it would be better for users to scroll page by page, I had to figure out how to situate them within the exhibit. How would they know which section they were on? What if they wanted to easily go back to the main Virtual Museum website? I preference tested three different formats to situate users:

  • a side menu that pops out once you click it
  • a stationary timeline on the side
  • a navbar that includes the Virtual Museum website

The side menu option proved to be the least obtrusive and most functional for users.

CHAPTER 12: The High-Fidelity Usability Testing

In the original prototype before testing, I didn’t incorporate arrows to show users how to get to the next part because two arrows (one for the menu popout and one to indicate the next section) looked too busy.

However, testing with Jessica showed me that an arrow was necessary. Because there were three arrows in the beginning to indicate to users that they must click or scroll to the next page, Jessica assumed that the side arrow was the arrow to use to go to the next page.

To fix this issue, I then incorporated a bottom arrow on each frame that you can see only once your mouse hovers over it.

PART 5: FINAL THOUGHTS

CHAPTER 13: The Epilogue

Testing showed positive reactions towards the product. Users enjoyed the look, feel, and engaging content in the Goya exhibit. The trivia component was a success.

Virtual Museum’s website was also well received. All three user testing showed that it was intuitive to use and explore through different exhibits.

If I could redo some things with Virtual Museum, I would conduct more research around the audience, especially as they relate to the COVID-19 pandemic since more people are at home.

Figuring this out could be especially helpful for marketing and branding for Virtual Museum.
